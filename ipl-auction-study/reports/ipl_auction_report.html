🔴 Critical Finding

In 94.4% of IPL seasons, the most expensive buy at auction failed to win any major individual award (Orange Cap, Purple Cap, or MVP). The only exception was Ben Stokes in 2017, who won MVP while playing for Rising Pune Supergiant after being bought for ₹14.5 Crore.

⚠️ Price Inflation, Not Performance Inflation

While average big-buy prices have doubled from ₹9.7Cr (Early era) to ₹19.5Cr (Modern era), average run production has actually declined from 231 to 198 runs.

✅ Best Value for Money

Kieron Pollard (2010) remains the best value big buy in IPL history with a value score of 109.0, purchased for just ₹4.8 Crore. He delivered incredible all-round performances that season.

09 — Methodology

How This Study Was Conducted

A transparent overview of the data pipeline and scoring methodology.

1

Data Collection

Ball-by-ball IPL match data (2008–2025) was sourced and the highest buy per season was manually curated with award winners.

2

Aggregation

Batting stats (runs, SR, 4s, 6s) and bowling stats (wickets, economy) were aggregated per player per season.

3

Name Matching

Fuzzy matching (surname-first + token sort ratio) was used to link award names with ball-by-ball data names.

4

Scoring Formula

Batting: Runs × (SR/100) if balls faced ≥ 50.
Bowling: Wickets × (8/Economy) if balls bowled ≥ 60.
Value Score: Raw Score ÷ Price (₹Cr).

📝 Limitations

This study considers only the single most expensive buy each season. Some seasons had multiple high-value buys. The scoring formula is simplified and does not capture fielding contributions, captaincy value, or team context. IPL 2020 data may have gaps due to the COVID-affected season.

Conclusion

Across 18 seasons and over ₹250 Crore spent on the biggest buys, the data tells a clear story: auction price does not predict award-winning performance.

Only 1 out of 18 big buys went on to win a major individual award. As prices have escalated — from ₹9.5 Crore (MS Dhoni, 2008) to ₹27 Crore (Rishabh Pant, 2025) — performance returns have not kept pace. The IPL auction remains a fascinating marketplace where perceived value often diverges sharply from delivered value.

"In cricket, as in markets, the price you pay is not always the value you receive."
